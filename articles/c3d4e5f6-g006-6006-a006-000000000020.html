Waterproof Cycling Jackets: Gore-Tex vs Alternatives

By Alex Reynolds | May 18, 2026

There is no misery quite like being soaked through at mile 40 of a 60-mile ride, with 20 miles of cold rain between you and a hot shower. A waterproof cycling jacket is the difference between a character-building sufferfest and an enjoyable ride in wet weather. But the market is flooded with confusing terminology: Gore-Tex, Gore-Tex Paclite, Gore-Tex ShakeDry, Pertex Shield, eVent—each promising to keep you dry while letting your sweat escape. The reality is that no jacket does both perfectly, and the right choice depends on your specific riding conditions, intensity, and budget. This guide cuts through the marketing to explain what you actually get at each price point and which jackets perform best for road cycling, gravel riding, and commuting.

Who This Is For (And Who It's Not)

Best for Gore-Tex Active: Road cyclists and gravel riders who ride hard in the rain and need maximum breathability. The 3-layer construction balances weight, packability, and durability. The Gore Wear C5 Active at $199 is the best entry point into Gore-Tex for cycling.

Not ideal for Gore-Tex Active: Budget-conscious riders who only ride in light rain. A $89 jacket will keep you dry for a one-hour commute just as well—breathability matters most on rides over 90 minutes.

Best for eVent (Showers Pass): Commuters and tourers who ride in sustained rain and need maximum ventilation. The core vents on the Elite 2.1 are genuinely useful for regulating temperature on climbs.

Not ideal for eVent: Weight-conscious road racers. At 310 grams, the Showers Pass is heavy and bulky compared to a 165-gram Rapha or 128-gram Castelli.

Best for Pertex Shield (Rapha): Fast road riders who want a lightweight, packable jacket that looks good off the bike. The Rapha Pro Team Lightweight packs into a jersey pocket and is the most stylish option.

Wash your waterproof jacket every 10-15 rides with a technical cleaner like Grangers Performance Wash ($12) or Nikwax Tech Wash ($10). Do not use regular detergent—it leaves residues that clog the membrane pores. After washing, tumble dry on low heat for 20 minutes—the heat reactivates the DWR coating. If water stops beading on the surface, reapply a DWR treatment. Store the jacket hanging (not folded) to prevent creasing and delamination of the membrane.

A cycling-specific jacket has features that make a real difference: a dropped tail to cover your lower back when leaning forward, longer sleeves to prevent wrist exposure, a higher collar, reflective elements, and a cut that fits your riding position without bunching at the shoulders. A hiking jacket will work in a pinch, but it will ride up at the back, the sleeves will be too short, and the hood (if present) will catch wind. If you ride in the rain regularly, a cycling-specific jacket is worth the premium.

Waterproof jackets have a membrane (like Gore-Tex or eVent) with taped seams that prevent water from entering through stitch holes. They are rated at 10,000mm HH or higher. Water-resistant jackets (also called "showerproof") use a DWR coating on the fabric surface but have no membrane and no taped seams. They will repel light rain for 20-30 minutes but will soak through in sustained rain. A water-resistant jacket is fine for intermittent showers; a waterproof jacket is necessary for all-day rain.

For cycling, pit zips are less useful than on hiking jackets because your arms are extended forward, which closes the zipper gap. Core vents or back vents are more effective for cycling. The Showers Pass Elite 2.1 has large core vents that work well. The Rapha Pro Team Jacket uses a two-way front zipper that allows you to vent from the bottom. If you ride at high intensity in the rain, mechanical ventilation (vents) is more effective than relying on the membrane's breathability alone.

Layer appropriately for the temperature, not the rain. A merino wool base layer (like the Icebreaker 175 Everyday, $80) is excellent because it insulates even when damp and resists odor. In temperatures above 55°F, you may only need a thin synthetic base layer. Avoid cotton at all costs—it absorbs moisture and conducts heat away from your body 25 times faster than dry cotton. The jacket itself should be your outermost layer. If you wear a backpack, consider a jacket with reinforced shoulders.

The industry is in transition. PFAS (per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances) are the chemicals that gave DWR coatings their legendary water repellency, but they are persistent environmental pollutants linked to health issues. Gore's new ePE membrane and PFAS-free DWR (launched in 2023) is used in the Arc'teryx Beta Jacket and is nearly as effective as the old chemistry but requires more frequent reapplication of DWR. Early reviews suggest the difference is noticeable but acceptable—you may need to re-treat your jacket more often. The environmental benefits are significant.

Four Hours in the Rain: How I Learned That Breathability Matters

In April 2023, I signed up for a 100-mile gravel event called the Rasputitsa in northern Vermont. The forecast called for steady rain and temperatures around 42°F (5.5°C). I wore a $60 polyurethane rain jacket I had used for commuting—it was waterproof, and I thought that was enough. By mile 25, I was as wet from the inside as I would have been from the outside. The jacket trapped every bit of sweat my body produced during the three major climbs, and the moisture had nowhere to go. By mile 50, I was shivering uncontrollably and had to drop out at the aid station. The race medic told me I was showing early signs of hypothermia. That experience taught me that waterproofness without breathability is dangerous in cold conditions. The following month, I bought a Gore-Tex ShakeDry jacket—the Castelli Idro 3 ($399)—and tested it on a rainy 4-hour ride across the same terrain. The difference was night and day: the ShakeDry membrane beaded water on the outside while allowing vapor to escape, and I finished the ride dry and warm. The jacket weighed just 128 grams and packed down to the size of a tennis ball. It was the most expensive piece of cycling clothing I had ever bought, and it was worth every penny.

Understanding Waterproof Ratings: The Hydrostatic Head Test

A fabric's waterproof rating is measured in millimeters of hydrostatic head (HH)—the height of a column of water the fabric can withstand before leaking. A rating of 5,000mm HH means the fabric can resist a 5-meter column of water pressure. For reference: light rain exerts about 1,000mm of pressure, heavy rain with wind exerts about 5,000-8,000mm, and the pressure of kneeling on wet ground or a backpack strap pressing against the jacket can reach 15,000-20,000mm. Most cycling jackets range from 10,000mm (adequate for rain) to 28,000mm (Gore-Tex Pro, suitable for extreme conditions). A jacket with a 10,000mm rating will keep you dry in steady rain but may wet out at pressure points (shoulders, elbows) after 2-3 hours. A 20,000mm+ rating is necessary for all-day rain. Equally important is breathability, measured in grams of water vapor per square meter per 24 hours (g/m²/24h). A rating of 10,000 g/m²/24h is adequate for low-intensity riding; 20,000+ is needed for high-intensity cycling. Gore-Tex Pro achieves 28,000mm HH and 25,000 g/m²/24h breathability. Pertex Shield is typically 20,000mm HH and 20,000 g/m²/24h. Budget PU jackets may be 10,000mm HH and only 5,000 g/m²/24h—waterproof but essentially a sweat bag.

Gore-Tex: The Different Variants Explained

Gore-Tex is not a single product but a family of membranes. Gore-Tex Paclite is the lightest and most packable variant, using a 2.5-layer construction with a protective inner layer. It is waterproof (28,000mm HH) and compact but has lower breathability and is less durable. The Pearl Izumi Quest Barrier Convertible Jacket ($175) uses Paclite and weighs 210 grams. Gore-Tex Active is designed for high-output activities and uses a 3-layer construction with a thinner membrane for maximum breathability. The Gore Wear C5 Gore-Tex Active Jacket ($199) weighs 210 grams and is designed specifically for cycling with a dropped tail and reflective elements. Gore-Tex Pro is the most durable variant, using a 3-layer construction with a rugged face fabric. It is overkill for most cycling—it is designed for mountaineering and backcountry skiing—but the 7mesh Copilot Jacket ($350) uses it for a rugged, all-conditions cycling jacket. Gore-Tex ShakeDry was a revolutionary technology that eliminated the face fabric entirely, using a membrane that permanently beaded water on its surface, eliminating the "wetting out" problem. ShakeDry was discontinued in 2023 due to PFAS (per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances) environmental concerns, but remaining stock of jackets like the Castelli Idro 3 and Gore Wear C7 ShakeDry are still available at some retailers. The PFAS issue is reshaping the entire waterproof jacket industry, with Gore announcing a new ePE (expanded polyethylene) membrane that is PFAS-free.

Gore-Tex Alternatives: Pertex Shield, eVent, and Futurelight

Pertex Shield, developed by Mitsui & Co., is a waterproof-breathable membrane used by brands like Rapha, Outdoor Research, and Montane. The Rapha Pro Team Lightweight Rain Jacket ($325) uses Pertex Shield and weighs 165 grams. It is more breathable than Gore-Tex Paclite and almost as light, with a 20,000mm HH rating. eVent is a direct-ventilation membrane that allows air to pass through without accumulating condensation, unlike Gore-Tex which requires body heat to drive moisture out. The Showers Pass Elite 2.1 ($229) uses eVent and is a favorite among Pacific Northwest commuters. The jacket includes massive core vents that can be opened while riding, making it one of the most breathable options for high-output riding. The North Face Futurelight uses a nanospinning process that creates a membrane with microscopic pores, offering high breathability at a lower weight. The North Face Apex Flex GTX ($190) applies Futurelight to a cycling-specific design. Polartec NeoShell is another alternative with excellent stretch and breathability, used by 7mesh in the Revelation Jacket ($300). For budget options, the Endura Hummvee Waterproof Jacket ($89) uses Endura's own 2.5-layer membrane with a 10,000mm HH rating—adequate for commuting and light rain.

Frequently Asked Questions

Wetting out occurs when the DWR (Durable Water Repellent) coating on the face fabric of a waterproof jacket wears off, and the outer fabric absorbs water. When this happens, the jacket still blocks rain (the membrane underneath is waterproof), but it stops breathing because the fabric is saturated, and you will feel cold and clammy even though you are dry. ShakeDry eliminated this problem by having no face fabric to wet out. Modern PFAS-free DWR coatings are less durable, so you may need to reapply DWR treatment (Nikwax TX.Direct, $12) every 10-15 washes.

Conclusion

Your ideal waterproof cycling jacket depends on your riding intensity and weather conditions. For high-intensity road riding in sustained rain, the Gore-Tex ShakeDry (if you can still find it) or the Rapha Pro Team Lightweight ($325) with Pertex Shield are the best performers. For commuting and touring, the Showers Pass Elite 2.1 ($229) with eVent and core vents offers unbeatable comfort. For budget-conscious riders, the Gore Wear C5 Active ($199) is the best value in genuine Gore-Tex for cycling. Two action items: (1) measure your chest and check the brand's size chart carefully—cycling jackets are cut slim, and layering requires room; (2) budget for a technical wash and DWR re-treatment kit ($20-$25), because maintaining your jacket is what keeps it performing year after year.
