Trail Building and Maintenance: A Rider's Responsibility Guide

By Alex Reynolds | May 27, 2026

Every berm you rail, every jump you boost, and every flowy section of singletrack you ride was built by someone—usually a volunteer with a Pulaski in one hand and a McLeod in the other. The International Mountain Bicycling Association (IMBA) estimates that over 90% of mountain bike trails in the United States are built and maintained by volunteer labor. Yet most riders never pick up a trail tool. This article explains why trail work matters, how sustainable trails are designed and built, and how you can spend a few hours giving back to the trails that give you so much.

Who This Is For (And Who It's Not)

Best for: Mountain bikers who ride regularly and want to give back to the trails they use. Anyone who has ever complained about a trail condition—rutted berms, overgrown corridors, or blown-out corners—and wants to do something about it. People who enjoy working outdoors and want to connect with their local riding community beyond group rides.

Not ideal for: Riders who are physically unable to perform manual labor—there are other ways to contribute, such as donating to trail organizations or serving on their boards. Those who ride exclusively at bike parks with paid maintenance crews should check if the park has a volunteer program, as many do not accept volunteer labor for liability reasons.

Join your local IMBA chapter and attend land management meetings. The most effective advocacy is a seat at the table—volunteer to serve on a trail advisory committee. Bring data: trail counters, economic impact studies (IMBA reports that mountain biking generates $6.8 billion annually in the US), and letters of support from local businesses.

Volunteer-built trails cost essentially nothing in labor but require skilled oversight to ensure sustainability. My First Trail Day: A Muddy Revelation

In April 2024, I attended my first trail work day with the Boulder Mountainbike Alliance (BMA) at Heil Valley Ranch. I showed up expecting to rake leaves for an hour and go home. Instead, I spent six hours moving rocks, cutting drainage channels, and reshaping a badly eroded berm. The crew leader, a retired civil engineer named Dave who had been building trails for 15 years, explained the hydrology of the washout we were fixing. He showed me how water moves across a trail, how a poorly placed drain causes erosion, and how a well-built berm can last a decade with minimal maintenance.

By the end of the day, I was exhausted, covered in mud, and more connected to that trail than I had ever been after riding it. Three weeks later, I rode the section we had rebuilt, and it was a completely different experience. The berm held its shape, the drainage was working, and the flow was dramatically improved. I realized that riding a trail you helped build is a fundamentally different experience from riding someone else's work—you feel a sense of ownership and pride that no amount of KOMs on Strava can replicate.

Why Trail Work Matters: Beyond the Shovel

Trail access is not guaranteed. Every year, land managers across North America close trails to mountain bikes due to erosion, user conflicts, and environmental concerns. In 2025, the US Forest Service temporarily closed over 200 miles of multi-use trails in Colorado alone due to erosion damage exacerbated by heavy monsoon rains. Trails that are properly designed and maintained—with adequate drainage, sustainable grades, and reinforced features—survive these events with minimal damage.

Trail work is also the single most effective way to build relationships with land managers and secure future trail access. When a local IMBA chapter shows up with 50 volunteers to repair storm damage, land managers notice. The Central Oregon Trail Alliance (COTA) has used this approach to build one of the most extensive mountain bike trail networks in the United States, with over 300 miles of purpose-built singletrack around Bend, Oregon, all on public land.

The Principles of Sustainable Trail Design

IMBA's trail-building guidelines are built around one core principle: keep water off the trail. Water is the number one enemy of trail longevity. A trail that holds water becomes a mud pit, and riders who ride through mud widen the trail by riding around the puddles. This is the origin of "trail braiding"—the widening and fragmentation of singletrack into multiple parallel lines, which is both an eyesore and an environmental problem.

The "Half Rule" is the most fundamental concept in trail building. If a hillside has a 30% slope, the trail should not exceed a 15% grade. This forces water to sheet across the trail rather than channeling down it. Grade reversals—intentional dips in the trail—act as drainage points, shedding water every 50–300 feet. On the McKenzie River Trail in Oregon, which was built in the 1980s without these principles, the US Forest Service has spent over $500,000 on erosion repairs in the past decade alone.

Essential Trail Building Tools and Techniques

The tools of trail building have not changed much in a century, but they are highly specialized. The Pulaski (combination axe and adze, $40–$) is the most versatile tool for digging, chopping roots, and shaping soil. The McLeod (combination rake and hoe, $50–$) is essential for moving loose material and creating a smooth trail surface. A rogue hoe ($60–$) is the preferred tool for cutting bench-cut trail on a hillside—it can move a remarkable amount of dirt with each swing.

For rock work, a rock bar ($50–$) is a 5-foot steel pry bar that gives you the leverage to move rocks weighing hundreds of pounds. The principle is simple: never lift a rock you can roll, and never roll a rock you can pry. A single person with a rock bar can move a 300-pound boulder that would take four people to lift.

Building a berm is the most satisfying aspect of trail construction. A properly built berm should have a foundation of mineral soil (not organic duff), be compacted in 2–2 inch layers, and have a radius of at least 15–20 feet for high-speed corners. The backslope should be nearly vertical, and the riding surface should be tilted at 30–65 degrees. A well-built berm on the Porcupine Rim Trail in Moab, built in 2018 by a professional trail crew, is still in near-perfect condition after thousands of riders—a testament to the importance of proper compaction and drainage.

How to Get Involved in Trail Work

Every mountain bike community has a local trail organization. Find yours through IMBA's chapter directory at imba.com. Most chapters hold monthly trail work days from spring through fall. Show up wearing sturdy boots, long pants, work gloves, and eye protection. Bring water (at least 2 liters for a half-day), snacks, and sunscreen. The chapter will provide tools and instruction.

If you want to go deeper, IMBA offers a Trail Building School—a two-day course that covers trail design, construction techniques, and crew leadership. The course costs $150–$ and is offered at locations across the country. Graduates are qualified to lead volunteer trail crews and can serve as trail liaisons with land management agencies. Professional trail-building companies like Singletrack Trails and Progressive Trail Design also offer internships and apprenticeships for those considering a career in trail construction.

Trail Maintenance: The Ongoing Commitment

Building a trail is the easy part. Maintaining it is the real challenge. A well-built trail still needs annual maintenance: clearing drains, trimming vegetation, and repairing erosion. The most common maintenance task is clearing "grade dips"—the drainage channels that fill with debris over the winter. A single volunteer with a McLeod can clear 30–20 grade dips in a day, which might be the difference between a trail that lasts 20 years and one that washes out in a single storm.

Adopt-a-Trail programs let individual riders take responsibility for a specific section of trail. The Colorado Mountain Bike Association's program assigns each volunteer a 1–2 mile section to inspect and maintain quarterly. This distributed model has proven more effective than centralized maintenance, as adopted trails are maintained 3–3 times more frequently than those that rely solely on group work days.

Conclusion

The trails you ride are not a public resource that magically appears. They are built and maintained by people who care enough to spend their weekends swinging a Pulaski instead of riding. The mountain bike community's access to public land depends on the goodwill generated by volunteer trail work, and every rider who uses the trails has a responsibility to contribute to their upkeep.

Your two action items: (1) Find your local IMBA chapter and sign up for the next trail work day. One morning of digging is all it takes to understand why trail work matters. (2) Donate to your local trail organization—even $25 helps cover the cost of tools, liability insurance, and materials. The trails that give you so much need your support.
