How to Prepare for Your First Gran Fondo

By Alex Reynolds | May 11, 2026

You have signed up for your first gran fondo—a 100+ mile mass-participation cycling event with timed segments, aid stations, and thousands of other riders. The registration confirmation email is in your inbox, and a mix of excitement and panic is setting in. How do you train for climbs you have never seen? What gear do you actually need to bring? How do you navigate the chaotic start corral with 3,000 riders? This article walks you through every step of gran fondo preparation—from the 10-week training plan to the event-day checklist—so you show up at the start line with confidence, not anxiety.

Who This Is For (And Who It's Not)

Best for: Road cyclists who have completed at least one 60-mile ride and are ready to step up to the challenge and spectacle of a mass-participation event. Riders who enjoy the social and competitive elements of organized events and want a goal to structure their training around.

Not ideal for: Complete beginners who have not yet ridden 50 miles. Build your base fitness first with a 50-mile training plan. Also, riders who dislike crowds and timed competition—if you would rather ride alone at your own pace, a gran fondo is not for you. The entry fees ($150–$) are also significant; consider whether the aid stations, timing chips, and closed roads are worth the price for your riding style.

The Gran Fondo National Series (granfondoguide.com) lists over 40 events across the United States. Major events include Levi's Gran Fondo (Santa Rosa, CA, October), Gran Fondo Hincapie (Chattanooga, TN, May), RBC Gran Fondo Whistler (Vancouver, BC, September), and the Alpine Loop Gran Fondo (Harrisonburg, VA, September). BikeReg.com and Active.com also list regional fondos. Most events open registration 6–3 months in advance.

For climbs averaging 6%+ with pitches above 12%, a compact crankset (50/34T) paired with an 11-34T cassette is the minimum recommended gearing. This gives you a 1:1 ratio (34/34) for the steepest sections. For very steep events like the Dolomites gran fondos in Italy, a sub-compact crankset (48/32T or 46/30T) with an 11-34T cassette is ideal. Spinning at 80 rpm in a 34/34 gear at 250 watts produces roughly 9.5 mph—manageable on a 12% grade. Grinding at 50 rpm in a 34/28 gear would require more torque and fatigue your legs faster.

Aid stations are not timed segments, but dawdling for 20 minutes will add significant time to your total. Target 5–10 minutes per stop. A grab-and-go strategy: refill both bottles, grab 2–3 food items to eat on the bike, use the restroom if needed, and get moving. Do not sit down—your muscles will stiffen. If you have a support crew, hand them a pre-packed musette bag with your preferred nutrition and fresh bottles for a 2-minute stop.

Every gran fondo has a SAG (Support and Gear) vehicle that sweeps the course behind the last rider. If you cannot continue, the SAG wagon will transport you and your bike to the finish area. There is also usually a time cutoff—riders who cannot maintain a minimum pace (typically 10–12 mph average) may be asked to take a shortcut or board the SAG wagon. Check the event's official cutoff time before registering. There is no shame in a DNF—the course will be there next year.

A power meter is ideal for pacing climbs because it responds instantly to effort changes, while heart rate lags by 30–45 seconds. On a 20-minute climb, power data lets you settle into your target wattage within the first 30 seconds. Heart rate alone will have you going too hard for the first 2–3 minutes while you wait for the reading to catch up. If you do not have a power meter, use perceived exertion and a heart rate cap: stay below 85% of your maximum heart rate on the first two climbs.

Three strategies: (1) Use a smart trainer with Zwift or TrainerRoad to simulate long climbs. The Alpe du Zwift is a 7.6-mile virtual climb at 8.5% average grade that takes 50–60 minutes and is excellent climbing practice. (2) Do over-geared intervals on flat roads—shift into a harder gear (53×14 or similar) and ride at 50–60 rpm while maintaining Sweet Spot power for 10–15 minutes. This simulates the muscular force demands of climbing. (3) Find the longest bridges, parking garages, or highway overpasses in your area and do hill repeats. A 0.2-mile bridge repeated 20 times gives you 4 miles of climbing. It is not glamorous, but it works.

My Levi's Gran Fondo Disaster (And Redemption)

In 2022, I signed up for Levi's Gran Fondo in Santa Rosa, California—a 103-mile course with 8,800 feet of climbing, including the infamous King Ridge Road. I lived in Virginia at the time, where my longest climb was 3 miles at 4%. I trained on rollers and thought I was ready. I was not. The first timed segment—a 7-mile climb up Coleman Valley Road with an average grade of 6.5% and pitches reaching 15%—destroyed me. I averaged 265 watts for the first 20 minutes, well above my FTP of 248, and cracked hard. I finished the climb in 52 minutes, but the remaining 70 miles were a death march. I crossed the finish line in 7 hours and 48 minutes, 23rd from last in my age group.

In 2024, I returned with a completely different approach. I had trained specifically for sustained climbing with a 10-week plan that included two weekly Sweet Spot sessions and a weekly long ride with 4,000–6,000 feet of climbing. I had studied the course profile on RideWithGPS and knew exactly when each timed segment started and ended. I paced the first climb at 220 watts—8% of my FTP—and had enough in the tank to actually race the final segment. I finished in 6 hours and 12 minutes, a 96-minute improvement, and placed in the top 25% of my age group. Here is exactly how I prepared.

Pacing the Timed Segments: The 90% Rule

Most gran fondos have 3–5 timed segments distributed across the course. The most common mistake—and the one I made in 2022—is attacking the first segment at 100–105% of FTP and then paying for it on the remaining 80 miles. The 90% rule is simple: ride the first two timed segments at 85–90% of your FTP. For a 250-watt FTP rider, that is 213–225 watts. Save your true race effort (95–100% of FTP) for the final segment only. This pacing strategy is supported by research on pacing in cycling time trials: a 2019 study in the European Journal of Applied Physiology found that a negative pacing strategy (starting below threshold and finishing above it) produced faster overall times and lower perceived exertion than even pacing in efforts lasting 60–90 minutes. The same principle applies to multi-segment fondo courses. Here is a pacing template for a fondo with four timed segments:

Between segments, ride at Zone 2 (56–75% of FTP) to recover. Do not stop at aid stations on timed segments—they are timed. Save your stops for the untimed sections between segments.

The 10-Week Gran Fondo Training Plan

A gran fondo demands a different training approach than a flat century. The defining feature is sustained climbing with timed segments, so your training must prioritize climbing-specific power and pacing. This plan assumes you can already ride 50 miles with 3,000 feet of climbing comfortably. All power references use a five-zone model based on FTP.

Sweet Spot intervals (88–94% of FTP) are the foundation of climbing preparation. They build muscular endurance at the exact intensity you will use on long climbs. Start at 88% of FTP and progress to 94% by week 8. If your FTP is 250 watts, your Sweet Spot range is 220–235 watts. Tempo intervals (76–90% of FTP) build the aerobic base that supports repeated climbing efforts. Do them at 80–85% of FTP, and focus on holding a steady cadence of 80–90 rpm throughout.

Gran Fondo Gear Checklist: What to Bring

A gran fondo is a self-supported event—there are aid stations, but you are responsible for your own mechanicals and basic needs. Here is the complete checklist, organized by where to carry it:

On the bike: Two full water bottles (750 ml each) with electrolyte mix. A saddle bag containing: two spare inner tubes, two tire levers, one CO2 inflator with two 16g cartridges, and a multi-tool with a chain breaker. A mini-pump as a backup if CO2 fails. A Garmin Edge or Wahoo computer with the full course loaded, including the timed segments marked as "segments" so you can see them approaching. In your jersey pockets: Phone in a waterproof case ($15), ID, health insurance card, $40 cash, and 6–8 energy gels or bars. If the forecast calls for rain, a lightweight packable jacket (Castelli Squadra Stretch, $119) that packs into a jersey pocket. On your body: Bib shorts with a high-density chamois (Assos, Rapha, or Castelli), a moisture-wicking jersey, gloves, sunglasses, and a helmet with a MIPS liner. Do not wear a new kit on event day—test everything on a training ride of at least 4 hours first.

Start Corral Strategy and Group Dynamics

Gran fondo start corrals are self-seeded and typically organized by expected finish time. Position yourself in the corral that matches your predicted pace, not your ego. If you expect to average 16 mph, join the 15–17 mph group, not the 18–20 mph group. Starting too far forward means you will be passed by hundreds of riders and will be tempted to ride above your target pace to "keep up." Starting too far back means you will spend the first 10 miles passing slower riders, which wastes energy and creates safety hazards. In the first 5 miles, the field will be dense—,000 riders packing onto two-lane roads. Ride predictably, hold your line, and do not overlap wheels. After 10 miles, the field thins out, and you can settle into a group that matches your pace. If you find a group riding at your target speed, sit in the draft and save 20–40% of your energy. If the group is too fast, let them go. Gran fondo regret is always about going out too hard, never about starting too conservatively.

Frequently Asked Questions

A century ride is a 100-mile ride, typically on open roads with no timing or competition. A gran fondo is a mass-participation event that includes timed segments (usually climbs), aid stations, mechanical support, and sometimes closed or partially closed roads. Gran fondos are more structured and competitive, but they are not races—the timed segments are for personal achievement, and riders of all levels participate. The entry fee ($150–$) covers support, food, and often a post-ride meal.

Conclusion

A gran fondo is one of the most rewarding experiences in amateur cycling—the combination of personal challenge, beautiful roads, and shared suffering with thousands of other riders creates memories that last far longer than the finishing time. The 10-week training plan, the 90% pacing rule, and the gear checklist above are your blueprint for a successful first event. Your two action items: (1) Study the course profile of your target fondo on RideWithGPS or Strava and identify every timed segment and major climb, and (2) Schedule your first Sweet Spot interval session this week—even if the event is months away, start building the muscular endurance you will need on those long climbs.
